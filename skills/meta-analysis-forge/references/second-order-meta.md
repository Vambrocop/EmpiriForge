# Second-Order Meta-Analysis and Umbrella Reviews

Use this reference when synthesizing existing reviews or existing meta-analyses rather than primary studies only.

## Terminology

- **Systematic review**: structured search and synthesis of studies.
- **First-order meta-analysis**: statistical pooling of primary study effects.
- **Umbrella review**: systematic review of reviews/meta-analyses.
- **Second-order meta-analysis**: statistical synthesis of meta-analytic estimates, with attention to overlap and dependency.

These terms are sometimes used inconsistently. Define exactly what the project does.

## Core Problem

Second-order synthesis risks double counting the same primary studies across multiple reviews. It also risks pooling estimates that used different inclusion criteria, effect-size transformations, models, or quality standards.

Always assess:

- overlap of primary studies;
- quality of included reviews;
- comparability of outcomes and effect metrics;
- date of last search;
- whether reviews include the same populations;
- whether newer reviews supersede older ones.

## Workflow

1. Define review-level question.
2. Search for systematic reviews and meta-analyses.
3. Extract review characteristics.
4. Extract pooled effects and uncertainty.
5. Build a citation matrix of primary studies by review.
6. Quantify overlap.
7. Decide whether to synthesize reviews statistically, narratively, or by choosing the highest-quality/current review.
8. If statistically synthesizing, model dependence and avoid naive double counting.
9. Grade certainty of evidence.
10. Report overlap, discordance, and limitations prominently.

## Overlap Diagnostics

Track:

- number of primary studies per review;
- shared primary studies across reviews;
- corrected covered area or similar overlap measure;
- whether the same primary study contributes multiple effects.

## Statistical Options

Depending on data availability:

- synthesize pooled effects from reviews with random effects, while acknowledging dependence;
- reconstruct primary-study level dataset when possible;
- use multilevel models with review and primary-study layers;
- use robust variance estimation for dependent estimates;
- avoid statistical pooling if overlap and heterogeneity make it misleading.

## Interpretation Rules

Do not claim stronger evidence merely because many reviews exist. Many overlapping reviews may represent repeated analysis of the same evidence base.

A useful umbrella review often contributes by:

- mapping evidence quality;
- identifying contradictory conclusions;
- showing where evidence is duplicated;
- separating robust findings from fragile ones;
- identifying domains needing primary research.
